PhillipsIoT

In my second semester of freshman year, I got to be a part of a research stream called Phillips Virtual Culture. In it, we were tasked to take the Phillips Collection art museum in Washington D.C. and see how we could use technology to improve the museum experience.

My project focused on improving one of the tedious tasks the museum had to perform, monitoring the humidity and temperature in each exhibit room.

The IoT device consisted of a Raspberry Pi Zero, which had a temperature/humidity sensor attached to it. The Pi would communicate the data recorded by the sensor to Adafruit's IoT platform.

The dashboard was made using Node.JS and Semantic UI. The front end was using Socket.IO for real time data updates from the IoT device. The backend was subscribed to Adafruit's IoT platform and broadcasted data to all connected clients whenever new data came in.

Cardr

At Bitcamp 2017, my team and I created Cardr. Cardr is an online e-wallet for your business cards. Its goal is to minimize the number of cards you have to carry around, but keeps all of your business cards, only a few clicks away.

When you receive a business card, you open up Cardr and snap a picture of it. Cardr uses Google CV to analyze the card and retrieve all essential information from it such as phone numbers, email addresses, and names. After it has gathered all of the information it can from the card, it creates a virtual business card that stores all of that information.

Cardr also helps you organize your cards by linking them to specific events and lets you search for cards based on those events. Another way to query info is to use our companion Alexa skill, which you can query for either the phone number or email address of an individual, from their name or company.

Terrapin Nav

As my internship was winding down, the summer before my freshman year, I had some free time to learn something on my own. I took this time, to teach myself how to develop Amazon Alexa Skills.

Terrapin Nav allows you to ask how long it would take to walk between any two buildings at UMD's campus. I thought this would be very helpful for the beginning of the year as students would not know what would be the optimal time to leave for class. A quick shout to Terrapin Nav would return exactly how long the walk would take.

The skill works by using UMD's student run api. This allowed me to get every building's exact latitude and longitude. From there, I fed the two sets of coordinates into Google's Geomatrix API, which spit it out navigation information between those two points, which I returned back to the user.

EzPill

My first season of Major League Hacking hackathons was a blast. I started with my first hackathon in November 2015 with HackRPI. From there, it's been a joy ride from exploring new campuses to meeting some really cool people. At the end of the 2016 season, my friend Vineet Shah and I were invited to the season finale, MLH Prime.

MLH Prime is a finale hackathon that is full of winners from that season. For Prime, we built EzPill. EzPill is a smart pill dispenser whose goal is to make managing medicine a breeze.

When you get your prescription, you would empty your pills into one of the two pill drums. Then, you would use our Android app to scan the label on your prescription which would load all of your prescription info into a calendar in our app. Next, you would be prompted to select a time at which you want to EzPill to dispense your pills. When it came time to take your pills, it would dispense the correct dosage and shoot you a text message you to remind you to take your medicine.

We keep track of how many pills were left through the companion app and a web app that stored info about all of your prescriptions that you could glance at if you were out shopping and didn't know if you needed a refill. To build EzPill, we utilized a lot of materials we found at Prime. We used cardboard, 3D printed parts, PVC, and servos for the physical device. The device was powered by a raspberry pi which synced with the web app and android app using Twilio's Sync platform.

Fireberry Pi

At Bitcamp 2016, my team and I created Fireberry Pi - A smart smoke and gas detector. The idea spawned from the statistic that over 500 deaths occur from gas leaks in homes every year and a time when a smoke detector went off in my home, but no one was there to respond.

Fireberry Pi fixes this issue by alerting all family members of smoke or gas leak through a text message or alert in our companion Android app. You would also receive a picture of the room with the text message so you can evaluate the situation on your own.

The project was built with a Raspberry Pi 3 and an Arduino Uno. The Uno was used as a micro controller which managed the input from our gas/smoke sensor. The Rasperry Pi is what connects all of our hardware to the web and Android app.

Lots of Holes

On the way to HackUMBC 2016, my team and I went over a lot of potholes. We were annoyed but didn't think much of them. Once we got to the hackathon however, we decided to look into the process of reporting potholes. We found out that in order for the county to fix potholes, the public had to call in and report them. We thought this was too much effort so we came up with Lots of Holes.

Lots of holes is an Android app that you let run in the background while you drive. Once the app detects you go over a pothole (Measurements from the accelerometer are monitored), a GPS flare is emitted to a Firebase DB. The DB then maps to a google maps layer that can be shared with the county.

The app works through crowdsourcing as the more people that report a pothole, the higher priority the pothole gets assigned. This is also used to help remove anomalies.

Terp Laundry

Terp Laundry is an Alexa skill that allows UMD students to check the status of washers and dryers in their dorm. I came up with the idea when a friend of mine complained that they had gone downstairs to the laundry room with their clothes, only to come back up after seeing that all the washers were currently in use.

The skill works with any dorm at UMD, even with dorms that have multiple laundry rooms. It currently uses Selenium running PhantomJS as a headless browser to authenticate with UMD and scrape the laundry room stats from their website. I am currently talking with UMD to get access directly to the API that provides information about the Laundry Machines on campus.

Galileo

Daemon Dash 2016 my team and I developed Galileo. Galileo is a schedule builder aimed at replacing Venus, our very old and clunky schedule generator, provided by UMD. Venus allows for no customization, preferences, or requests and spits out randomly generated permutations of the classes you want to sign up for.

We wanted to make the schedule generator smarter and cater more towards the students needs so we wrote our own version that includes filters and preferences. For example, students would be able to specify their dorms and get back a schedule that would be the most optimal in terms of walking.
